# Unity

Unity is a men's Bible study designed to help thoughtful Christians walk hand-in-hand when we disagree.

The study is not built around winning arguments or revealing the leader's preferred position. It is built around learning where sincere Christian disagreements come from, practicing gracious conversation, and becoming better readers of Scripture together.

## Purpose

Unity has three core aims:

1. Understand where different beliefs that thoughtful Christians hold come from.
2. Practice gracious discussion, especially when we disagree.
3. Learn how to read the Bible more carefully, as a servant to charity and understanding.

The main thrust is unity without flattening conviction. Participants should leave better able to say, "I understand why a faithful Christian might read this differently," even when they still hold their own position.

## Guiding Posture

Each topic should be taught in a way that:

- Steel-mans each major position before evaluating it.
- Uses primary sources wherever possible.
- Gives all meaningful positions their best biblical, theological, historical, and pastoral arguments.
- Avoids signaling the teacher's personal view.
- Separates the authority of Scripture from the authority of our interpretation.
- Names the difference between first-order gospel issues, serious but secondary doctrinal issues, and matters where faithful Christians have long disagreed.
- Encourages men to ask better questions before offering better rebuttals.

## Desired Group Culture

Unity should cultivate:

- Humility without apathy.
- Conviction without contempt.
- Careful reading without weaponizing technical knowledge.
- Patience with men who are newer to Scripture or church history.
- Courage to state disagreement clearly and kindly.
- A habit of asking, "What is the strongest version of the view I do not hold?"

## Session Format

Each week should eventually include:

- A PowerPoint presentation.
- A leader guide.
- A participant handout or discussion sheet.
- A source research folder with quotes, links, and interpretive notes.

Suggested flow for a session:

1. Frame the purpose of the discussion and remind the group of the Unity posture.
2. Read the central biblical text aloud.
3. Identify the interpretive pressure points.
4. Present Position A in its strongest form.
5. Present Position B in its strongest form.
6. Add historical and theological context.
7. Practice discussion with guided questions.
8. Close with what both positions are trying to honor.

## Research Standards

Research notes should:

- Prefer primary sources over summaries.
- Provide links for every source that can be linked.
- Keep quotes short and checkable.
- Include enough citation detail to verify the quote later.
- Distinguish direct quotation from paraphrase.
- Note when a source is an advocate, critic, translation, or secondary summary.
- Preserve uncertainty rather than smoothing it over.
- Include a self-review section when the topic is especially contested.

Acceptable source categories include:

- Biblical texts and original-language tools.
- Early church, medieval, Reformation, and later theological writings.
- Confessions, catechisms, and denominational resources.
- Commentaries and monographs.
- Articles by advocates of each position.
- Relevant archaeology, history, science, or philosophy sources when they illuminate interpretive claims.

## Neutrality Practices

To avoid revealing or over-weighting the leader's personal position:

- Present positions in rotating order across weeks.
- Use the same slide structure for competing views.
- Give each position roughly comparable care and speaking time.
- Avoid labels that prejudge the issue, such as "literal view" versus "compromise view."
- Use charitable names the advocates themselves would recognize.
- Say "advocates argue" instead of "the Bible clearly says" when presenting contested readings.
- Include strengths and cautions for each view.
- End with shared Christian commitments rather than a verdict, unless a topic truly requires one.

## First Topic: The Flood

The first planned topic is the flood in Genesis, especially the question:

> Should Christians understand the flood as global, regional/local, or is the text doing something that does not map neatly onto that modern question?

The initial research lives in [`flood/`](flood/).

Planned files:

- `overview.md` - topic map, positions, and presentation direction.
- `biblical-and-ane.md` - biblical texts, Hebrew terms, ancient Near Eastern context, and Jewish reception.
- `historical-reception.md` - early church, medieval, Reformation, and later Christian interpretation.
- `global-flood-position.md` - steel-man of global flood readings.
- `regional-flood-position.md` - steel-man of regional/local or flexible readings.
- `discussion-guide.md` - questions and facilitation notes.
- `source-audit.md` - verification checklist, quote audit, and unresolved source questions.

## Working Principle

The study should make disagreement feel less like a threat and more like an invitation to read carefully, speak truthfully, and love one another well.
