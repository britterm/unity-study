Lesson 1
The Flood: Differing views on Genesis 6-9

Start by stating the purpose plainly. This is not a study to tell the men what to think about the flood. It is a study to inform them about what faithful Christian camps think, why they think it, and how to be gracious with each other when they disagree.

WHY ARE WE HERE

We are here to build a unified backbone for our brotherhood.

  • We are going to learn why brothers believe differently on a variety of topics.
  • We are going to practice brotherly respect.
This is a universal blueprint for how we handle disagreement. We are not here to vote, flatten differences, or pressure an immediate conclusion. We are learning content and practicing character simultaneously.

Charity and grace

A man can hold one conviction strongly and still represent another view fairly.

  • Charity is part of Christian maturity.
  • Accurate listening is a spiritual discipline.
  • Fair representation is a way of loving the truth.
Charity is not politeness layered over doctrine. It is part of discipleship and godly manhood.

How we will speak

Our conversation covenant

  1. Use familiar and gracious terminology.
  2. Try to understand the strongest version of a view.
  3. Do not belittle a brother because he holds a different interpretation.

The first act of charity is accurate listening.

The flood can become a proxy fight over trust in Scripture, science, or modernity. Set the conversational covenant before opening the debate.

WHAT TO AVOID

We are not voting on who believes the Bible.

We will NOT

  • make non-essentials the essentials
  • caricature any view
  • use slogans instead of arguments

We WILL

  • read Scripture carefully
  • identify real tensions
  • discuss which claims are essential and which are debated

The Theological Core

What is the Holy Spirit teaching us?

“The earth was corrupt in God's sight, and the earth was filled with violence.” — Genesis 6:11

1. Holy Justice

The Flood is not a random natural disaster. It is a direct, holy response to the catastrophic weight of human evil.

2. Sovereign Mercy

Before the water ever drops, God promises a covenant to preserve life. Judgment and grace are bound together.

“But I will establish my covenant with you, and you shall come into the ark...”
Genesis 6:18

The Textual Tension

Recognizing the tensions within the narrative

Faithful men disagree because the text itself holds two real realities in tension.

The Cosmic Language

The text repeatedly uses absolute, universal phrases like “under the whole heaven” and “all flesh” to demand a massive, totalizing scope.

The Ancient Lens

Localized, eyewitness semantics intended to frame the event within the human horizon. “Earth” (erets) in the original context carries the specific sense of “land” or “country.”

Building a unified backbone means understanding the tension rather than forcing a shallow solution.

Use this slide to show the men that the friction is right there in the text. One side is guarding the absolute, totalizing rhetoric; the other is guarding the ancient, localized semantics. Both are trying to be faithful to the words on the page.

Summary of the views

Three readings commonly appear among thoughtful Christians.

Global flood

The flood covered the whole earth and destroyed all land life outside the ark.

Regional/local flood

The flood was geographically bounded but universal in human and theological scope.

Flexible/literary-theological

The flood was a historical crisis recounted through cosmic symbolism to emphasize creation-reversal and covenant.

These are categories for understanding, not boxes for judging brothers.

Must this text be read dogmatically?

We refuse to compromise on the truths Scripture makes completely clear.

  • God has spoken truly and authoritatively.
  • God is Creator of all things.
  • Humanity is fallen and accountable.
  • God judges evil and saves by grace through Christ.

The Global Flood Reading

A Planet Reshaped by Judgment & Mercy

The Plain Meaning, the Scope of the Ark, and the Cosmic Horizon

As we strive for unity, our goal tonight is to fully appreciate the theological beauty and textual strength of the global reading. Let's look at this view through the eyes of those who hold it with deep devotion.

01 / Textual Weight

The Totality of the Language

The primary strength of the global view is its direct alignment with the comprehensive language chosen by the Holy Spirit throughout the narrative.

"All flesh in which is the breath of life under heaven."
"All the high mountains under the whole heaven were covered."
"Everything on the dry land... died."

Genesis 6:17; 7:19-23

02 / Narrative Harmony

The Purpose of the Ark & Covenant

Proponents find that a global scope provides the most seamless, straightforward explanation for the specific details of the account.

The Scale of the Ark

The immense size of the vessel and the 120-year build point to a crisis of planetary proportions. If the danger were localized, a simple migration out of the region would have sufficed to preserve life.

The Scope of the Promise

God’s covenant never to send such a flood again is tied directly to the rainbow. A global scope ensures that this sacred promise remains unbroken, as no universal flood has ever recurred.

Mortenson | Morris

03 / Canonical Links

The Pattern of Cosmic History

The New Testament authors consistently weave the global scale of Noah’s flood into their theology of the final judgment.

  • Jesus (Matthew 24): Draws a parallel between the absolute, life-altering sweep of the flood in Noah’s day and the literal, inescapable nature of His future Second Coming.
  • Peter (2 Peter 3): Explicitly pairs the original de-creation of the globe by water with the future cosmic restoration of the creation by fire.
"The global view honors the pattern of Scripture: a universal physical judgment in history mirrors a universal physical judgment in the future."

04 / The Record

Historical Roots & Physical Wonders

This position seeks to integrate the rich history of Christian interpretation with the striking features of the physical earth.

An Unbroken Legacy

For centuries, the classical Christian tradition—including pillars like Augustine, Luther, and Calvin—read the narrative at face value, treasuring its plain historical and theological reality.

A Catastrophic Footprint

Proponents point to vast sedimentary layers, rapid burial signs, and marine fossils atop high mountain peaks as remarkable physical landmarks of a world-shaping hydraulic event.

Snelling | Calvin

05 / Core Conviction

The Heart of the Position

At its core, the global view is driven by a deep desire to trust the most apparent, face-value meaning of the biblical text and to safeguard the grand typological patterns of Scripture.

Textual Trust

Receiving the universal language of the narrative with simple, straightforward faith.

Theological Clarity

Keeping the historical type perfectly aligned with the global scale of future prophecy.

Shared Legacy

Standing in continuity with the historic interpretation held by generations of the global Church.

The Regional Flood Reading

Geographically Bounded, Theologically Universal

An Eyewitness Account of Total Judgment and Covenant Mercy

As we move to the regional view, we must remember: proponents do not doubt God's power to flood the planet. They are motivated by a desire to respect the linguistic boundaries of ancient Hebrew and the worldview of the original audience.

01 / The Eyewitness Lens

Phenomenological Language

This view holds that the Bible describes the flood exactly as it appeared to the human eyes of Noah—a catastrophic event that filled the entire visible horizon.

  • The "Known World": In the ancient Near East, phrases like "under the whole heaven" typically referred to the entire inhabited world of the speaker, not a modern spherical globe.
  • Total in Judgment: While geographically regional, the judgment was theologically universal: it reached and judged every human being intended by the divine decree.
Proponents argue this isn't "minimizing" the flood, but rather "localizing" it to the actual world of the human race at that time.

02 / Linguistic Foundation

The Meaning of "Erets"

The debate is not about what God could do, but about what the Hebrew word erets (land/earth) meant to a writer in 1400 BC.

A Consistent Usage

The word erets occurs over 2,500 times in the Old Testament. In the vast majority of cases, it refers to a specific "land," "country," or "territory" (e.g., the Land of Israel).

Avoiding Anachronism

Regional proponents seek to avoid reading our modern, post-NASA concept of a "Planetary Sphere" back into an ancient text that used "earth" to mean the ground under one's feet.

03 / Scriptural Precedent

The Language of Totality

Scripture frequently uses "whole world" language to describe regional events of massive significance.

Genesis 41:57

"All the earth came to Egypt... to buy grain." In this context, "all the earth" clearly refers to the entire ancient Near Eastern world affected by the famine, not every continent on the globe.

Deuteronomy 2:25

God promises to put the fear of Israel on peoples "under the whole heaven." This identical phrase from the flood narrative is used here to describe the nations surrounding Canaan.

"The Bible uses absolute language to emphasize the utter completeness of a specific event."

04 / Internal Logic

The Ark as Witness & Mercy

If the flood was regional, why build an Ark? Proponents see the Ark as a theological necessity rather than just a survival craft.

A Visible Sermon

The building of the Ark served as a decades-long public witness and a warning of judgment. Migration would have been a quiet escape; the Ark was a physical testimony to Noah's world.

A New Beginning

The animals preserved were likely the domestic livestock and regional wildlife essential for Noah to "jumpstart" human civilization and the local ecology immediately after the waters receded.

The rainbow covenant is seen as a promise never again to destroy the human world (the civilization of that era) with a judgment of water.

05 / Conclusion

The Heart of the Position

Ultimately, the regional view is driven by a desire for exegetical honesty—trying to see the flood through the eyes of the ancient author to understand the intended scope of the judgment.

Hebrew Context

Honoring the standard Old Testament usage of geographic scope words.

Ancient Lens

Respecting the standard Near Eastern idioms used to describe total destruction.

Judicial Scope

Focusing on the total judgment of the people group targeted by the divine decree.

The Literary-Theological Reading

A Historical Crisis in Cosmic Language

The Architecture of De-Creation, Re-Creation, and Covenant Memory

As we look at our third view, our goal is to see past modern debates over water volume. Proponents of this view—including many conservative evangelical scholars—invite us to look at the deep structural patterns of Genesis to discover what the Holy Spirit is primarily emphasizing.

01 / The Theological Canvas

Beyond a Simple Journal

This view holds that Genesis presents a real, historical crisis, but intentionally clothes it in highly stylized, cosmic language to reveal the character of God.

  • Inspired Literary Art: The narrative is a beautifully structured masterpiece designed to teach theological truth, not just chronologically record physical measurements.
  • The Core Focus: The text isn't trying to satisfy modern scientific curiosity regarding geography; it is showing the world how God handles a creation ruined by human rebellion.

02 / Literary Architecture

The Symmetrical Heart of the Story

The entire flood narrative is arranged as a massive literary Chiasm—a design where matching pairs point to a single, crucial focal point at the exact center.

A: Noah and his family enter the safety of the Ark (7:13)

B: The waters rise, triumphing over the land for 150 days (7:17-24)

★ "And God remembered Noah" (8:1)

B': The waters recede, losing their grip on the land for 150 days (8:1-14)

A': Noah and his family exit the Ark onto dry ground (8:15-19)

The design demonstrates that the author's primary intent is to highlight God's covenantal faithfulness to His people amidst judgment.

03 / Cosmic Patterns

The Flood as a Systematic "Undo" of Genesis 1

Proponents urge us to look past physical mechanics and witness the profound theological imagery of a collapsing creation.

Creation (Gen 1) God restrains the chaotic waters, establishing cosmic boundaries so that dry land can emerge for life.
De-Creation (Gen 7) God safely pauses the system. The fountains burst and the windows open. The boundaries collapse, and creation temporarily returns to dark water.
Re-Creation (Gen 8-9) God sends His Ruach (Wind/Spirit) over the deep. Dry land emerges, and Noah steps forward as a "New Adam" to receive the creation mandate.

04 / New Testament Legacy

How the Apostles Read Noah

When New Testament authors refer to the flood, they routinely focus on its symbolic, archetypal, and pastoral power.

2 Peter 3 / The Final Reality

Peter draws directly from the cosmic imagery of the flood. The historical de-creation of the world by water serves as the ultimate theological blueprint for the future purification of creation by fire.

1 Peter 3 / The Picture of Salvation

Peter pulls the Ark into his gospel presentation as an explicit picture of salvation. Passing safely through the waters of death into new life directly mirrors the reality of Christian baptism.

05 / Core Conviction

The Heart of the Position

At its core, this view is driven by a deep reverence for the artistic craftsmanship of Scripture, choosing to focus primarily on the spiritual and covenantal landmarks established by the text.

Historical Reality

Affirming a real Noah, a real ark, and a historical cataclysm, while recognizing that the story is told through highly elevated theological language.

Creation Symmetry

Focusing on the purposeful themes of de-creation and re-creation that beautifully anchor the narrative to Genesis 1.

Spiritual Primacy

Prioritizing the rich symbolic architecture of the covenant sign and God's faithfulness over modern scientific specs.

Common Themes

What Must We Carry Together?

Unity is not compromise. It is anchoring ourselves to the undeniable core of God's Word while extending grace on the rest.

Shared Confession

The Non-Negotiable Bedrock

When we look past debates over geography or mechanics, all orthodox, Bible-believing men stand shoulder-to-shoulder on the massive truths of Genesis 6–9.

Sin is Catastrophic

Human evil is not a minor flaw; it actively corrupts creation and rightly provokes a holy, divine response.

Judgment is Historic

God is not passive. He enters human history to execute real, inescapable justice against unrepentant rebellion.

Grace is Preserving

Salvation is entirely initiated by God. Noah found unmerited favor in the eyes of a merciful Lord.

The Flood is not a children's nursery fable. It is a terrifying and beautiful monument to the Holiness and Mercy of God.

The Conversation Covenant

Iron Sharpening Iron

"He that answereth a matter before he heareth it, it is folly and shame unto him." — Proverbs 18:13

The Ground Rule

Break into your groups. To guard our unity and build empathy, don't critique a view until you have first tried to support it.

The Practice

  • Lean Out: Pick the view you are furthest away from.
  • Understand: Use your handout to genuinely explain that view to your table.
  • Appreciate: Explicitly name what that view beautifully protects.
  • Discuss: Only then, open Bibles and charitably talk through the tensions.

The Mark of Maturity

Conviction and Charity Together

Immature thinking demands that we flatten out every secondary disagreement into a test of orthodoxy just to feel secure. Mature men can hold deep, unwavering textual convictions while fiercely loving the brother across the table.

Final Discussion Prompt

What do you understand better—and appreciate more—about a view you do not personally hold?
