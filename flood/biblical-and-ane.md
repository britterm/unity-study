# Biblical And Ancient Near Eastern Research

This note gathers biblical texts, original-language pressure points, ancient Near Eastern flood traditions, and Second Temple / early Jewish reception material relevant to the global-or-regional flood discussion.

## Core Genesis Text

Primary biblical text:

- [Genesis 6-9, NRSVUE](https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Genesis+6-9&version=NRSVUE)
- [Genesis 6, Hebrew/English at Sefaria](https://www.sefaria.org/Genesis.6)
- [Genesis 7:19, NET with Hebrew notes](https://classic.net.bible.org/verse.php?book=Gen&chapter=7&verse=19)
- [Genesis 7:19, Bible Hub interlinear](https://biblehub.com/interlinear/genesis/7-19.htm)

Key scope-pressure passages:

- [Genesis 6:17](https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Genesis+6%3A17&version=NRSVUE): flood comes "on the earth" and destroys "all flesh" under heaven. Translation note: Hebrew `ha'aretz` can mean earth, land, ground, or country depending on context.
- [Genesis 7:19-20](https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Genesis+7%3A19-20&version=NRSVUE): waters cover "all the high mountains" under heaven. This is the strongest textual pressure toward a global reading.
- [Genesis 7:21-23](https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Genesis+7%3A21-23&version=NRSVUE): repeated totalizing language: all flesh, everything, every living thing, only Noah and those with him remain.
- [Genesis 8:4-5](https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Genesis+8%3A4-5&version=NRSVUE): the ark rests on the mountains of Ararat, and mountain tops are seen later.
- [Genesis 9:8-17](https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Genesis+9%3A8-17&version=NRSVUE): covenant with Noah, his descendants, and every living creature; no future flood will destroy all flesh.

Literary-theological observations:

- The flood reverses creation: waters above and below are released, dry land disappears, then the world is renewed. Compare [Genesis 1](https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Genesis+1&version=NRSVUE) with [Genesis 7:11](https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Genesis+7%3A11&version=NRSVUE).
- Human evil and violence are the moral reason for judgment. See [Genesis 6:5, 11-13](https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Genesis+6%3A5%2C11-13&version=NRSVUE).
- Noah functions as a new-Adam figure after the flood. Compare [Genesis 1:28](https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Genesis+1%3A28&version=NRSVUE) and [Genesis 9:1-7](https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Genesis+9%3A1-7&version=NRSVUE).

## Other Biblical Flood References

- [Isaiah 54:9-10](https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Isaiah+54%3A9-10&version=NRSVUE): the "waters of Noah" become covenant imagery for God's enduring mercy.
- [Ezekiel 14:14, 20](https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Ezekiel+14%3A14%2C20&version=NRSVUE): Noah is listed with Daniel/Danel and Job as an exemplar of righteousness.
- [1 Chronicles 1:4](https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=1+Chronicles+1%3A4&version=NRSVUE) and [Luke 3:36](https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Luke+3%3A36&version=NRSVUE): Noah in genealogical memory.
- [Matthew 24:37-39](https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Matthew+24%3A37-39&version=NRSVUE) and [Luke 17:26-27](https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Luke+17%3A26-27&version=NRSVUE): Jesus uses Noah's days as an analogy for sudden judgment.
- [Hebrews 11:7](https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Hebrews+11%3A7&version=NRSVUE): Noah as an exemplar of faith.
- [1 Peter 3:18-22](https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=1+Peter+3%3A18-22&version=NRSVUE): ark and waters are used typologically in relation to salvation and baptism.
- [2 Peter 2:5](https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=2+Peter+2%3A5&version=NRSVUE): Noah as a herald/preacher of righteousness; wording varies by translation.
- [2 Peter 3:5-7](https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=2+Peter+3%3A5-7&version=NRSVUE): flood judgment is paired with future judgment by fire.

## Original-Language Pressure Points

### `erets`

Hebrew `erets` can refer to earth, land, ground, country, or territory. The debate is not whether the word can mean "land"; it plainly can. The question is whether Genesis 6-9 supplies enough contextual markers to require the larger meaning.

Global-flood use:

- The repeated pairing of `erets` language with "all flesh," "under heaven," and "every living thing" appears to expand the scope.
- The covenant in Genesis 9 is with Noah's descendants and living creatures, which appears more than merely local.

Regional/local use:

- The word's normal semantic range permits "land" where context warrants.
- Biblical universal language elsewhere can be domain-limited, such as famine or royal-visitor language.
- Genesis may describe the whole human world in view rather than a modern globe.

### Universal Language

Words like all, every, whole, and under heaven are the main textual pressure point. Global readings emphasize their repetition. Regional/local readings ask whether ancient narrative often uses totalizing language from the narrator's horizon.

## Ancient Near Eastern Parallels

These sources should not be used to flatten Genesis into "just another flood myth." Their best use is comparative: they show a shared ancient flood-tradition world while highlighting Genesis' distinctive theology.

### Sumerian Flood Story / Eridu Genesis

Primary/near-primary links:

- [ETCSL t.1.7.4, The Flood Story](https://etcsl.orinst.ox.ac.uk/cgi-bin/etcsl.cgi?text=t.1.7.4)
- [Livius, The Great Flood: Eridu Genesis](https://www.livius.org/articles/misc/great-flood/flood2-t/)

Notes:

- Fragmentary Sumerian account involving Zi-ud-sura/Ziusudra.
- Includes divine decision, warning, flood, boat, seven days and nights, sacrifice, and settlement in Dilmun.
- ETCSL has the warning that "the seed of mankind is to be destroyed"; quote is translation-dependent and should be checked before slide use.

Steel-man uses:

- Global reading: ancient flood traditions also remember a massive, humanity-threatening event.
- Regional/local reading: the story-world is recognizably Mesopotamian, and Livius notes that a historical local flood in southern Babylonia cannot be excluded.

### Atrahasis

Primary/near-primary link:

- [Livius, The Great Flood: Atrahasis](https://www.livius.org/articles/misc/great-flood/flood3_t-arahasis/)

Notes:

- Old Babylonian core; Livius describes it as the fullest Mesopotamian flood account.
- Human noise disturbs the gods; Enlil sends a flood; Enki warns Atrahasis; after the flood the gods institute population-control measures.
- Short checkable excerpt: "build a boat" appears in Enki's warning. Keep any quotation short and note that the Livius version is adapted from B. R. Foster.

Theological contrast with Genesis:

- Atrahasis: polytheistic divine irritation/noise problem.
- Genesis: one God judges moral violence and corruption, preserves a remnant, and establishes covenant.

### Gilgamesh Tablet XI

Primary/near-primary links:

- [AncientTexts.org, Epic of Gilgamesh Tablet XI](https://www.ancienttexts.org/library/mesopotamian/gilgamesh/tab11.htm)
- [Livius, The Great Flood: Gilgamesh](https://www.livius.org/articles/misc/great-flood/flood3_t-gilgamesh/)

Notes:

- Utnapishtim recounts the flood to Gilgamesh.
- Includes divine council, secret warning, boat construction, loading living beings, storm, mountain landing, bird releases, sacrifice, and post-flood divine response.
- Short excerpt from the AncientTexts/Kovacs translation: "Tear down the house and build a boat." Translation-dependent.

Steel-man uses:

- Global reading: striking overlap with Genesis supports an ancient memory of a real catastrophic flood.
- Regional/local reading: named Mesopotamian geography such as Shuruppak and Euphrates naturally supports a regional setting for the tradition.

### Berossus

Primary/near-primary link:

- [Livius, The Great Flood: Berossus](https://www.livius.org/articles/misc/great-flood/flood3_t-berossus/)

Notes:

- Berossus is a Hellenistic Babylonian priest whose flood material survives in fragments through later writers.
- Useful as reception of Mesopotamian flood traditions into the Greco-Roman world.
- Use cautiously because this is fragmentary and mediated.

## Second Temple And Early Jewish Reception

### Josephus

Source:

- [Josephus, Antiquities of the Jews 1.3](https://penelope.uchicago.edu/josephus/ant-1.html)

Notes:

- Josephus retells Genesis and reports traditions about ark remains in Armenia.
- He cites other ancient writers, including Berossus/Nicolaus, as witnesses to flood tradition.
- This is reception evidence, not independent proof of Genesis' event.

### Jubilees

Source:

- [Jubilees index, R. H. Charles translation](https://sacred-texts.com/bib/jub/index.htm)
- [Jubilees 5:1-20](https://sacred-texts.com/bib/jub/jub18.htm)
- [Jubilees 5:21-32](https://sacred-texts.com/bib/jub/jub19.htm)

Notes:

- Rewrites Genesis with Watchers, heavenly tablets, precise chronology, Ararat/Lubar, covenant, and calendar interests.
- Often intensifies universal language; translation should be checked before quoting.

### 1 Enoch

Sources:

- [1 Enoch 10, R. H. Charles translation](https://sacred-texts.com/bib/boe/boe013.htm)
- [1 Enoch 106-107 / Book of Noah fragment](https://sacred-texts.com/bib/boe/boe110.htm)

Notes:

- Expands Genesis 6:1-4 with Watcher traditions.
- Uriel warns Noah of coming destruction.
- Useful for how Jewish interpreters connected the flood with angelic rebellion, corruption, and cleansing.

### Genesis Apocryphon

Source:

- [Genesis Apocryphon overview](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genesis_Apocryphon)

- Stable free scholarly translation is harder to cite; use a published edition for any serious quotation.

## Bottom Line For The Study

Global-flood advocates have their strongest textual case in Genesis' repeated totalizing language, the ark, the covenant, and the New Testament's typological use of Noah as a paradigm for final judgment.

Regional/local advocates have their strongest textual-contextual case in the semantic range of `erets`, ancient phenomenological language, examples of context-limited "all" language, and the Mesopotamian setting of ancient flood traditions.

Both readings can affirm the theological center of the passage: human violence, divine judgment, gracious preservation, covenant mercy, and new creation.
