# Flood Discussion Guide

Use this as a first-pass facilitation guide. It should remain position-neutral and should help the group practice fair summary before critique.

## Opening Reminder

The goal is not to identify which men are on which side. The goal is to understand why faithful Christians have read this passage differently and to practice speaking with conviction and charity.

## Warm-Up Questions

- What parts of the flood account feel most central to the passage's message?
- What questions do you naturally bring to the text?
- Which questions might be modern questions rather than the passage's main concern?

## Text Observation Questions

- What repeated words or phrases do you notice in Genesis 6-9?
- What does the passage emphasize about human sin?
- What does it emphasize about God's judgment?
- What does it emphasize about God's mercy?
- What does the covenant with Noah include?

## Steel-Man Practice

- What is the strongest biblical argument for a global flood?
- What is the strongest biblical argument for a regional/local flood?
- What is one concern each position is trying to honor?
- What is one weakness or unresolved question each position must face?

## Gracious Disagreement Practice

- Can you summarize the other position in a way an advocate would recognize?
- What would you need to understand better before disagreeing strongly?
- Which parts of the issue are central to the gospel, and which are secondary?
- How can we avoid making our interpretation a test of fellowship where Scripture does not require it?

## Closing Prompt

Name one thing you understand better about a view you do not hold.

## Leader Neutrality Notes

- Do not ask for a show of hands on who holds which view.
- Avoid saying one position is "just taking the Bible seriously."
- Avoid saying one position is "just following science."
- When someone makes a strong claim, ask them to name the concern behind the other view before responding.
- If the conversation gets abstract, return to Genesis 6-9 and ask what the text emphasizes most.

## Possible Final Summary

Both major readings are trying to honor something important.

The global reading is trying to honor the repeated universal language, the ark, the covenant, historical reception, and the New Testament's use of Noah as a paradigm of judgment and salvation.

The regional/local reading is trying to honor Hebrew word range, ancient context, phenomenological language, and the difference between the text's theological message and modern geography questions.

The shared center is not small: God judges evil, preserves by grace, makes covenant, and calls his people to trust him.
