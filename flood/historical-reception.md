# Historical Reception Research

This note gathers early church, medieval, and Reformation reception of Noah's flood. The most important interpretive caution: many sources assume universal language, but they are usually not answering the modern global-versus-regional question directly. They are more often asking what the flood teaches about sin, judgment, baptism, Christ, the Church, and final judgment.

## Cross-Period Patterns

- Universal/global language is common in major Christian interpreters.
- Typology is equally common: ark as Church, waters as baptism/judgment, Noah as faith and obedience.
- Literal and allegorical readings were often combined, especially in Augustine.
- The older tradition generally treats the flood as real history, but does not build a modern scientific flood model.
- Use historical reception as precedent and theological framing, not as a shortcut that ends the conversation.

## Justin Martyr, Mid-2nd Century

Source:

- [Justin Martyr, Dialogue with Trypho 19, New Advent](https://www.newadvent.org/fathers/01282.htm)

Notes:

- Justin uses Noah mainly in a covenantal/apologetic argument about righteous pre-Mosaic figures.
- He does not develop flood geography in this passage.

Fair use in the study:

- Useful for Noah as part of salvation-history and covenant discussion.
- Not a strong source for a developed global/local position.

## 1 Clement, Late 1st Century

Source:

- [1 Clement 9, Early Christian Writings](https://www.earlychristianwritings.com/text/1clement-hoole.html)

Short quote:

> "Noah, having been found faithful..."

Notes:

- 1 Clement presents Noah as faithful and connected to renewal/regeneration of the world.
- The focus is moral example and divine preservation.

Fair use in the study:

- Shows very early Christian use of Noah as an obedience/faith exemplar.
- Does not settle geography.

## Irenaeus, Late 2nd Century

Sources:

- [Irenaeus, Against Heresies 4.36, New Advent](https://www.newadvent.org/fathers/0103436.htm)
- [Irenaeus, Against Heresies 5.29, New Advent](https://www.newadvent.org/fathers/0103529.htm)

Short quote:

> "in the days of Noah"

Notes:

- Irenaeus treats the flood as real divine judgment.
- He uses Noah's flood in relation to disobedience, judgment, and God's one economy of salvation.
- Some Irenaeus passages use strong universal language, such as deluge and whole-earth framing.

Fair use in the study:

- Helpful for showing an early universal judgment reading.
- Also helps keep the emphasis theological rather than merely geographic.

## Tertullian, Early 3rd Century

Sources:

- [Tertullian, On Baptism, Tertullian Project](https://www.tertullian.org/works/de_baptismo.htm)
- [Tertullian, Concerning Baptism 8, Souter translation](https://www.tertullian.org/articles/souter_orat_bapt/souter_orat_bapt_04baptism.htm)

Short quote:

> "the Church...was represented by the ark"

Notes:

- Tertullian connects the flood to baptism.
- He describes the flood as a kind of baptism of the world and the dove as a sign of peace after judgment.
- His primary interest is sacramental typology, not geography.

Fair use in the study:

- Excellent source for showing that early Christians often moved from flood history to baptismal and ecclesial meaning.
- Avoid presenting him as a technical global-flood source.

## Origen, Early-Mid 3rd Century

Source:

- [Origen, Homilies on Genesis and Exodus, Internet Archive record](https://archive.org/details/origenhomilieson0000orig)

Notes:

- Origen famously layers allegorical and moral readings over the ark and flood narrative.
- He is a major witness for reading the flood spiritually without necessarily denying the narrative.
- Online primary access is limited; use a published translation before quoting.

Fair use in the study:

- Use Origen to show the tradition was not simply "literal or nothing."
- Do not turn Origen into a modern nonliteralist without checking exact passages.

## John Chrysostom, Late 4th Century

Sources:

- [Chrysostom on Genesis 9, Bible Hub excerpts](https://biblehub.com/commentaries/chrysostom/genesis/9.htm)
- [Homilies on Genesis bibliographic record, Princeton](https://byzantine.lib.princeton.edu/byzantine/translation/15112)
- [Homilies on Genesis 18-45, PDF found online](https://www.catholicapologetics.info/scripture/oldtestament/Homilies%20on%20Genesis%2018-45%20-%20Chrysostom%2C%20John%2C%20St_.pdf)

Notes:

- Chrysostom is generally plain-sense, pastoral, and moral in his Genesis preaching.
- He stresses Noah's obedience, God's patience, human wickedness, and judgment.
- Use exact quotes only from a verified translation.

Fair use in the study:

- Good for ordinary Antiochene reception: flood as real divine judgment and moral warning.
- Avoid making him answer modern geology.

## Augustine, Early 5th Century

Sources:

- [Augustine, City of God 15, New Advent](https://www.newadvent.org/fathers/120115.htm)
- [Augustine, City of God 16, New Advent](https://www.newadvent.org/fathers/120116.htm)

Short quote:

> "a figure of the city of God"

Notes:

- Augustine strongly defends the historicity of the ark and flood.
- He also insists the ark signifies Christ and the Church.
- In City of God 15.26-27 he discusses ark dimensions, symbolism, and objections to bare history without allegory or allegory without history.
- Augustine's approach is not "literal versus spiritual"; it is both historical and typological.

Fair use in the study:

- Augustine is one of the best historical sources for a balanced ancient posture: event, symbol, Christ, Church.
- He supports a traditional universal reading, but his main concern is theological meaning.

## Theodoret Of Cyrus, 5th Century

Source:

- [Patrologia Graeca 80, Internet Archive](https://archive.org/details/patrologiaecurs77migngoog/page/n5/mode/2up)

Notes:

- Theodoret's Questions on Genesis belongs to the Antiochene question-and-answer tradition.
- English online access is limited.

Fair use in the study:

- Mention as part of broad Antiochene reception only if needed.
- Do not rely heavily without a checked translation.

## Bede, Early 8th Century

Source:

- [Bede: On Genesis, Liverpool University Press bibliographic page](https://www.liverpooluniversitypress.co.uk/doi/book/10.3828/978-0-85323-672-0)

Notes:

- Bede receives Augustine heavily.
- He reads the flood historically and typologically: ark as Church, floodwaters in relation to baptismal judgment/salvation.
- Stable free primary text is limited.

Fair use in the study:

- Useful to show medieval continuity with Augustine.
- Cite bibliographically unless a checked translation is available.

## Thomas Aquinas, 13th Century

Sources:

- [Aquinas, Summa Theologiae I, q. 73, New Advent](https://www.newadvent.org/summa/1073.htm)
- [Aquinas, Catena Aurea on Matthew 24, ecatholic2000](https://www.ecatholic2000.com/catena/untitled-31.shtml)

Notes:

- Aquinas does not offer a full Genesis commentary comparable to Augustine or Calvin.
- In reception, the flood appears through providence, judgment, patristic catenae, and sacramental theology.

Fair use in the study:

- Best used for medieval continuity, not as a detailed flood-geography source.

## Martin Luther, 16th Century

Sources:

- [Luther on Genesis, Lutheran Library PDF](https://www.lutheranlibrary.org/pdf/356-luther-genesis.pdf)
- [Luther on Genesis, Lutheran Library page](https://www.lutheranlibrary.org/356-luther-genesis/)
- [Luther, Commentary on Genesis Vol. II, PDF found online](https://oldmis.kp.ac.rw/admin/admin_panel/kp_lms/files/digital/Core%20Books/Theology/Commentary-on-Genesis-Vol-II-Luther-on-Sin-and-the-Flood.pdf)

Notes:

- Luther reads the flood as historical, universal judgment.
- He emphasizes Noah's faith, preaching, obedience, suffering, and God's promise.
- Use a checked public-domain edition before quoting exact wording.

Fair use in the study:

- Strong Reformation witness for a universal reading.
- His main concern is faith, judgment, promise, and unbelief rather than a scientific model.

## John Calvin, 16th Century

Sources:

- [Calvin, Commentary on Genesis 7, Bible Hub](https://biblehub.com/commentaries/calvin/genesis/7.htm)
- [Calvin, Commentary on Genesis, CCEL PDF](https://www.ccel.org/ccel/c/calvin/calcom01/cache/calcom01.pdf)

Short quote:

> "the whole world was immersed"

Notes:

- Calvin reads the flood historically and takes universal language seriously.
- On Genesis 7 he stresses that the flood was divine judgment, not fortune or ordinary natural disaster.
- He also sees de-creation imagery: God removes the barriers that normally restrain the waters.

Fair use in the study:

- Calvin can represent classic Protestant universal-flood reception.
- Present him as an exegete and theologian, not as a modern flood-geology writer.

## Presentation Use

For slides, this historical material should probably be presented in three buckets:

1. Early and medieval Christians commonly treated the flood as real divine judgment.
2. Many used universal language, but few were debating modern global/local geography.
3. The flood was usually read typologically: Christ, Church, baptism, salvation, and final judgment.

Neutral phrasing:

> "The broad historical reception leans toward a universal reading, but its center of gravity is theological and typological rather than geological."
