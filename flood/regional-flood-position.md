# Regional Or Local Flood Position

## Position Summary

The flood in Genesis 6-9 may have been geographically regional or local while still being universal in theological and human scope. It judged the whole corrupted human world in view, preserved Noah's family, and serves as a true biblical account of judgment, salvation, and covenant.

A strong non-global reading does not have to deny Scripture, miracles, judgment, or Noah. The best advocates argue that Genesis is either describing a real catastrophic regional flood in universal/hyperbolic language, or describing a flood universal to the narrator's theological world rather than to a modern planetary map.

## Strongest Steel-Man

Regional/local advocates often see themselves as guarding careful attention to language, ancient context, and the difference between the Bible's theological claim and later scientific questions. They are not necessarily trying to make Genesis "less miraculous"; many are trying to read it as ancient Scripture rather than as a modern geography report.

## Argument 1: `erets` Can Mean Land

Primary biblical text:

- [Genesis 6-9, NRSVUE](https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Genesis+6-9&version=NRSVUE)
- [Genesis 7:19, Bible Hub interlinear](https://biblehub.com/interlinear/genesis/7-19.htm)

Advocate sources:

- [Carol A. Hill, "The Noachian Flood: Universal or Local?" ASA / PSCF PDF](https://www.asa3.org/ASA/PSCF/2002/PSCF9-02Hill.pdf)
- [Reasons to Believe, "Biblical Foundation for RTB's Flood Model"](https://reasons.org/explore/publications/nrtb-e-zine/biblical-foundation-for-rtb-s-flood-model)
- [Michael Heiser, "How To Argue From the Biblical Text for a Local-Regional Flood"](https://drmsh.com/argue-biblical-text-local-regional-flood-instead-global-flood/)

Claim:

- Hebrew `erets` can mean earth, land, ground, country, or territory.
- The debate is whether Genesis 6-9 context requires planet-wide scope.
- Regional/local advocates argue that "land" remains a plausible reading in at least some flood passages.

Steel-man phrasing:

> "The regional view does not begin by deleting words like 'all' and 'earth'; it begins by asking how those words work in Hebrew narrative."

## Argument 2: Biblical Universal Language Can Be Context-Limited

Primary comparison texts:

- [Genesis 41:57](https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Genesis+41%3A57&version=NRSVUE)
- [1 Kings 10:24](https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=1+Kings+10%3A24&version=NRSVUE)

Advocate sources:

- [Reasons to Believe, "Biblical Foundation for RTB's Flood Model"](https://reasons.org/explore/publications/nrtb-e-zine/biblical-foundation-for-rtb-s-flood-model)
- [Carol Hill, ASA / PSCF](https://www.asa3.org/ASA/PSCF/2002/PSCF9-02Hill.pdf)

Claim:

- Scripture sometimes uses all/whole-world language in ways that are contextually bounded.
- Genesis 41:57 says all the earth came to Egypt for food; regional advocates argue this need not mean every person on the globe.
- Therefore, "all the earth" in Genesis 6-9 may mean the whole relevant human world.

Gracious note:

- Global advocates respond that Genesis 7:19-23 is more comprehensive than these comparison texts. This is a real point to represent fairly.

## Argument 3: Ancient Phenomenological Language

Advocate sources:

- [BioLogos interview with John Walton and Tremper Longman](https://biologos.org/articles/the-genesis-flood-through-ancient-eyes-an-interview-with-john-walton-and-tremper-longman)
- [IVP, The Lost World of the Flood](https://www.ivpress.com/the-lost-world-of-the-flood)
- [Michael Heiser, local-regional flood article](https://drmsh.com/argue-biblical-text-local-regional-flood-instead-global-flood/)

Notes:

- Ancient writers often describe events from an observer's horizon rather than from a satellite view.
- Walton and Longman argue that Genesis can describe a real regional catastrophe in cosmic terms because the event represents judgment, de-creation, and re-creation.
- In the BioLogos interview, Longman says "A flood did happen," and describes Genesis 1-11 as theological history using figurative language. Walton argues that biblical authority is honored by reading according to literary form.

Steel-man phrasing:

> "If the author used hyperbole to make a theological point, then reading the hyperbole as hyperbole is not evading the text; it is trying to respect the text's form."

## Argument 4: Theological Scope Does Not Require Planetary Scope

Advocate sources:

- [Reasons to Believe, "Biblical Foundation for RTB's Flood Model"](https://reasons.org/explore/publications/nrtb-e-zine/biblical-foundation-for-rtb-s-flood-model)
- [Hugh Ross, "Exploring the Extent of the Flood: Part Two"](https://reasons.org/explore/publications/nrtb-e-zine/exploring-the-extent-of-the-flood-what-the-bible-says-part-two)
- [BioLogos, "Genesis and the Flood: Understanding the Biblical Story"](https://biologos.org/articles/genesis-and-the-flood-understanding-the-biblical-story)

Claim:

- The flood may be universal with respect to humanity under judgment, even if not global in geography.
- RTB argues that if humanity had not yet spread across every continent, a geographically local flood could still be anthropologically universal.
- The central theological claims remain: sin, judgment, grace, preservation, covenant.

## Argument 5: Why Build An Ark?

Advocate source:

- [Hugh Ross, "Why Build an Ark?" Reasons to Believe](https://reasons.org/explore/publications/reasons-newsletter/why-build-an-ark)

Claim:

- Regional/local advocates acknowledge the force of the ark objection.
- Ross argues that the ark functioned as public prophetic warning and a visible platform for Noah's righteousness, not merely as transportation.
- The ark also dramatized the sincerity of the offer of mercy.

Steel-man phrasing:

> "On this reading, the ark is not an inefficient evacuation plan; it is a public sign of coming judgment and offered mercy."

Research file:

Sources:

- [ETCSL, The Flood Story](https://etcsl.orinst.ox.ac.uk/cgi-bin/etcsl.cgi?text=t.1.7.4)
- [Livius, Eridu Genesis](https://www.livius.org/articles/misc/great-flood/flood2-t/)
- [Livius, Atrahasis](https://www.livius.org/articles/misc/great-flood/flood3_t-arahasis/)
- [AncientTexts.org, Gilgamesh Tablet XI](https://www.ancienttexts.org/library/mesopotamian/gilgamesh/tab11.htm)

Claim:

- Ancient flood traditions are strongly tied to Mesopotamian geography and memory.
- Genesis may engage a known ancient flood tradition while transforming its theology.
- This supports the plausibility of a regional background without forcing Genesis to be merely derivative.

## Argument 7: Archaeology And Geology Raise Real Questions

Advocate sources:

- [Carol Hill, ASA / PSCF](https://www.asa3.org/ASA/PSCF/2002/PSCF9-02Hill.pdf)
- [IVP, The Lost World of the Flood](https://www.ivpress.com/the-lost-world-of-the-flood)
- [BioLogos interview with Walton and Longman](https://biologos.org/articles/the-genesis-flood-through-ancient-eyes-an-interview-with-john-walton-and-tremper-longman)

Claim:

- Some Christians argue that geology, archaeology, animal distribution, and human population history make a recent global flood difficult to reconcile with the record of nature.
- Walton and Longman include geological concerns but frame the argument hermeneutically: what sort of literature is Genesis 6-9?

Facilitation caution:

- Use science gently. The study should not imply that science gets to overrule Scripture. The stronger presentation is that God's world may prompt us to reread Scripture more carefully.

## Gracious Discussion Notes

This view often sees itself as guarding:

- Careful attention to Hebrew words and ancient genre.
- The difference between theological universality and geographical modernity.
- A high view of Scripture read according to literary form.
- Honesty about nature, history, and archaeology.
- The central message of the flood: sin, judgment, mercy, covenant.

Best discussion question:

> "What concern is the regional/local reading trying to protect, and where do you see that concern in the text?"
